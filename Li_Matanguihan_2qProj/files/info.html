The History of DBSA

The beginnings of DBSA go way back to when it was still the Manic Depressive Association (MDA) in 1978. The MDA hosted its first convention in Chicago in 1984, where approximately 50 leaders from 14 states were in attendance. 2 years later in 1986, the MDA became the NDMDA (National Depression and Manic Depression Association) and started using "Honk If You're On Lithium" as its promotional slogan. In 1994, NDMDA published its first large-scale self-survey for the members of its chapters. NDMDA becomes replaced with DBSA in 2003 to reflect modern psychiatric terminology. Then, in 2010, DBSA begins advancing its depression and bipolar research to learn more about the disorders that cause so much trouble on the human minds. In 2016, DBSA created its first online support groups to help those who lack easy access to the local support groups. DBSA launches its website in 2019 which then proves to be a great help as soon as 2020 rolls around, allowing them to give support to people even amidst the Covid Pandemic. (DBSA, 2022)

The Mission and Vision

Mission

DBSA provides hope, help, support, and education to improve the lives of people who have mood disorders.

Vision

The Depression and Bipolar Support Alliance (DBSA) envisions wellness for people living with mood disorders (depression and bipolar disorder).

Core Beliefs

DBSA Believes:

  1. Mood disorders can be treated and people with mood disorders do exist.
  2. Everything DBSA does is influenced by the experiences of the peoplethey are helping.
  3. DBSA recognizes several pathways to treatment including peer support, personal wellness strategies, therapeutic and medical interventions. All individuals have the right to direct their own treatment.
  4. Support from peers is important in the treatment of mood disorders.
  5. Having the peer perspective at the center of conversations about mental health results in better outcomes.
  6. Wellness encompasses an individual's whole life, and each person's wellness journey is unique.
  7. DBSA provides support for all individuals living with or affected by mood disorders at all points in their wellness journey.
  8. Clinicians should collaborate with peers to provide options when developing personalizes treatment plans that target complete wellness, not merely improvement.
  9. Researchers should work to provide treatment options that target complete wellness, not merely improvement.
  10. All people should have access to quality mental health care.
